Test and Scale

Explore options cheaply, then commit decisively — especially when reporting logic, definitions or service models are changing.

The principle explained

Test and Scale means exploring multiple possible routes to success in a cheap, safe, repeatable way, then moving decisively once the evidence is clear.

In the expedition metaphor, the team does not send the whole group onto dangerous ice to find out whether it is safe. They use drones, probes, route markers and small tests to identify the best route. But testing is only half the principle. Once the right route is clear, the team must be ready to move quickly and commit properly — promoting prototypes into products, not leaving pilots running forever.

I would not treat a new data model as automatically meaning the same thing in a different format. I would test whether it still answers the service question — then scale what works.

The principle in practice

Test and Scale is how I would avoid betting the whole six-month secondment on the first plausible solution. I would run small tests with real users, compare old and new logic side by side, and be willing to stop ideas that look tidy but mislead — especially while definitions are still moving during migration.

A good Business & Performance approach does not mean building a permanent dashboard for every suggestion. It means keeping a visible portfolio of ideas, deciding what deserves further investment, and scaling only when managers actually use the output to make decisions.

Definition migration — three options tested

Problem: PathwayOne stores referrals as actions, not cases. The new system’s default count is not automatically the right answer for the service or for NHSE returns.

What I would do: Test OPT-A, OPT-B and OPT-C against a known anchor patient and a full March reconciliation — old system, new default, agreed logic and dashboard — before embedding anything in the warehouse.

Who: Clinical lead on rejected-referral logic; BI on extraction; mandatory reporting owner on NHSE alignment; performance lead on sign-off.

Artefact: Three options and March reconciliation (151 / 168 / 134 / 154).

Benefit: The agreed figure is defensible in a directorate workshop, not “whatever the feed produced”. After six months: The decision log and reconciliation method transfer to the next definition dispute.

MHSDS-like submission test

Problem: Mandatory returns can be submitted on legacy logic while local teams have already moved to a new model — then NHSE query arrives months later.

What I would do: Build the submission from the same episode fact as local products, run pre-submission validation checks, and compare agreed OPT-C to what the dashboard would have submitted.

Who: Mandatory reporting owner, BI lead, finance on activity alignment where relevant.

Artefact: MHSDS-like mock submission with VAL-MH checks and reconciliation summary.

Benefit: Submit 134 when agreed, not 154 because the dashboard refreshed. After six months: Inclusion rules and checks are documented, not tacit.

Source-to-report map filters

Problem: A full lineage map is overwhelming for service managers; a static PDF goes stale the week after export.

What I would do: Test preset filters (mandatory vs local, low-confidence, migration-risk rows) with CMHT leads in short walkthrough sessions — do they answer real questions without BI translation?

Who: Two CMHT leads for pilot feedback; BI maintains the underlying CSV; performance lead facilitates the sessions.

Artefact: Interactive source-to-report map with filter presets.

Benefit: Managers can follow a figure back in one sitting. After six months: Filters become the standard onboarding tool for new report owners.

Weekly demand/capacity pack

Problem: Service managers juggle several spreadsheets for referrals, waits, workforce and agency — none aligned on the same definition in migration month.

What I would do: Prototype one weekly pack with two CMHT leads for six weeks; compare meeting quality and decision speed against their old spreadsheet routine; dismiss a naive forecast when it overshoots 18% during definition change.

Who: CMHT service managers, workforce lead, finance BP on agency lines.

Artefact: Demand, capacity and productivity insight (promoted from pilot — IDEA-01).

Benefit: One shared view for capacity conversations; INS-03 stopped a misleading forecast reaching the Board. After six months: Pack owned by performance lead with CMHT sign-off, not a secondment side project.

Reporting assurance merge

Problem: Risk register, validation outputs and confidence model told the same story three different ways — sponsors read one page and miss the publish gate.

What I would do: Merge three registers into one narrative (risks → checks → publish decision), review with BI and performance leads, then promote as the single assurance product.

Who: BI lead, performance lead, directorate sponsor for sign-off on withhold rules.

Artefact: Reporting assurance during migration (IDEA-02 promoted).

Benefit: Clearer “what we published and what we withheld” story for March 2026. After six months: One assurance rhythm replaces three overlapping registers.

Ideas under test register

Problem: Good suggestions become permanent reports by default; failed pilots linger as “work in progress” with no honest stop.

What I would do: Keep a visible register of promoted, under test, parked and dismissed ideas — including manager 1:1 guides, DNA recovery pilots and statistical forecasting parked until data is clean.

Who: Performance lead curates; service managers and HR BP co-own people-focused pilots; BI supports data tests only where needed.

Artefact: Full ideas under test portfolio with plain-English test descriptions.

Benefit: Panel and directors see judgment, not just output volume. After six months: Register shows what to sustain, what to park and what not to repeat.
